About Me

My love for science and technology bloomed at an early age because it provided reliable answers to all the questions I had about how the world works and why it works that way. A very fortunate byproduct of this was my introduction and subsequent enamourment with science fiction, a long running and passionate affair, that is equal parts dreamy amazement at the possibilities the future might hold and frustration at all the scientific inaccuracies and artistic liberties taken. Of all the fields the one that I loved the most was physics, because it combined mundane and extraordinary topics in one beautiful (to my eyes at least) package.

My first career choice as a tutor came naturally. The same drive I had to understand why and how the natural world works also made me want to reduce these explanations to their essential components, anything less than a clean and easy to understand answer always felt wrong. So teaching physics and mathematics was a way to both challenge myself to come up with better cleaner and easier to understand answers than the ones I was given as well as to help pass on the love I felt for the subject to a new generation. At some point however freelancing as a tutor was no longer financially sustainable, so I turned to another great passion of mine, computers.

Doing a masters in information technology and working as a software engineer was not just a way to switch to a career in a field that I also enjoyed, it provided me with a rare opportunity to explore my creative side in a way that physics never did. The joy of seeing a piece of software that you conceived and built from scratch being used is, in my opinion, something similar to what a craftsman feels when creating an artifact from start to finish. It has a rare and surprisingly alluring quality to it that has me hooked and wanting more.

My Story

  • My story so far.
    Physicist! Teacher! Software Engineer! Greek!

    Georgios Manakanatas

  • It is widely believed that modern workers will need to change multiple careers during their working lives. Being a scientist I had to validate the hypothesis by experiment, and since no one I knew seemed to be prepared to volunteer to be my test subject that left me with only one option: I would be my own guinea pig! So a few years ago I set out to start a new career and see how things would go, for science of course!

  • My background was a diploma in Physics from the University of Crete. My first profesional steps where as a tutor of Physics and Mathematics working both freelance and in collaboration with various tutoring centers in Heraclion and Athens in Greece. In paralel however I had always been involved with compluters as a hobby and when time came to switch careers this interest allong with my good mathematical background from my diploma made the IT field an easy choice

  • After some failed starts trying to find a part time master's cource that would not clash with my working schedule I found the perfect match in the Hellenic Open University. Three years lots of coffee and long nights later I was armed with an M.Sc. in Information Science, knowledge of Software Design and Management Processing, basic coding skills as well as data mining methods and tools from my Thesis. With these I started testing the hypothesis that someone would actually pay me to apply these new skills in practice.

  • Predictably the opportunity presented itself in a manner consistent with murphy's law. The job I found ( and have to this day ) in data integration put none of my fledgling skills in data mining, python and C to use and required a completely new set presenting a new and steep learning curve. Familiarising myself with a new set of middleware frameworks and tools as well as new languages on the job was often hectic and always stressful but after a year of working in the field I am now confident in my abilities.

  • While it's far too early to tell if my experiment will be sucessfull ,one year is hardly a career after all, it seems to be going well. I will of course continue to toil away in the service of science and hope that my sacrifices will one say be recognized.
